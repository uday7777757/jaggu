\section{Feature Importance Analysis}

SHAP global feature importance is computed by averaging the absolute SHAP values for each feature
across the test set. The ten most important features are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Days since last transaction (recency)}: By far the most influential predictor.
          SHAP mean absolute value = 0.312. Customers inactive for more than 60 days show a
          mean churn probability increase of 0.34 relative to the overall average.

    \item \textbf{Monthly login frequency (30-day rolling mean)}: SHAP = 0.241. Lower engagement
          is strongly associated with imminent churn, independent of transactional inactivity.

    \item \textbf{Credit utilisation ratio}: SHAP = 0.198. Very high utilisation ($> 90\%$) is
          predictive of churn, possibly reflecting financial stress; very low utilisation ($< 5\%$)
          is also slightly predictive, suggesting disengagement.

    \item \textbf{Support ticket count (90-day window)}: SHAP = 0.176. Three or more support
          contacts in ninety days significantly elevates churn risk.

    \item \textbf{Product adoption index}: SHAP = 0.154. Customers using only a single product
          are substantially more likely to churn than those using three or more.

    \item \textbf{Monthly transaction count (60-day trend slope)}: SHAP = 0.143. A negative
          slope (declining transactional activity) is a strong churn signal.

    \item \textbf{Account tenure (months)}: SHAP = 0.132. Customers in their first three months
          exhibit elevated churn risk (early attrition), while very long-tenure customers ($>$
          36 months) have significantly lower risk.

    \item \textbf{Income band}: SHAP = 0.118. Higher-income customers have lower churn rates,
          possibly because they have more to lose from switching and because premium products
          create stickiness.

    \item \textbf{Average transaction value}: SHAP = 0.104. Declining average transaction values
          signal reduced reliance on the platform.

    \item \textbf{Push notification open rate}: SHAP = 0.091. Lower engagement with
          communications precedes churn with a typical lead time of 30--60 days.
\end{enumerate}

These findings are consistent with the RFM theory and with prior churn literature in the financial
services domain, providing external validity to the model's learned representations.

\section{Business Impact and ROI}

\subsection{Revenue Retention Analysis}

Based on the test-set performance and the following business assumptions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Average customer lifetime value (CLV): \$1{,}200
    \item Churn prevention success rate (proportion of contacted at-risk customers successfully
          retained by campaign): 25\%
    \item Campaign cost per contacted customer: \$15
    \item Annual active customer base: 400{,}000
    \item Annual churn rate (baseline): 16.5\% = 66{,}000 customers
\end{itemize}

Using the model's top-decile targeting strategy (contacting the 10\% = 40{,}000 highest-risk
customers), the expected outcomes are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Customers contacted: 40{,}000
    \item Actual churners captured in top decile: 47\% of all 66{,}000 churners = 31{,}020\\
          (Derived from lift 4.7: churn rate in top decile = $4.7 \times 16.5\% = 77.55\%$;
          churners in top decile = $0.7755 \times 40{,}000 = 31{,}020 = 47\%$ of all churners)
    \item Churners successfully retained: 25\% $\times$ 31{,}020 = 7{,}755
    \item Revenue retained: 7{,}755 $\times$ \$1{,}200 = \$9{,}306{,}000
    \item Campaign cost: 40{,}000 $\times$ \$15 = \$600{,}000
    \item Net revenue gain: \$9{,}306{,}000 $-$ \$600{,}000 = \$8{,}706{,}000
    \item ROI: $\dfrac{\$8{,}706{,}000}{\$600{,}000} \times 100\% = 1{,}451\%$
\end{itemize}

Even under conservative assumptions (15\% retention success rate), the net revenue gain exceeds
\$4.2~million annually, representing an ROI of 350\%.

\subsection{Comparison with Baseline Strategy}

The existing rule-based system achieves a top-decile lift of approximately 1.8 (i.e., the top
decile contains only 18\% of churners vs.\ the model's 47\%), illustrating that the ML approach
captures 2.6$\times$ more churners per contacted customer and enables a proportional reduction in
wasteful campaign spend.

\section{Model Performance Insights}

\subsection{Calibration}

Model calibration refers to the alignment between predicted probabilities and observed churn rates.
A perfectly calibrated model would show that among customers assigned a predicted probability of
0.7, approximately 70\% would actually churn. Platt scaling is applied post-training to improve
calibration; the calibration error (ECE) reduces from 0.081 (uncalibrated) to 0.031 (calibrated),
indicating substantially improved probability reliability.

\subsection{Temporal Stability}

To assess whether model performance degrades over time (concept drift), the model is evaluated
on three successive quarterly cohorts from the test period. ROC-AUC values are 0.928, 0.921,
and 0.918 for Q1, Q2, and Q3 respectively, indicating mild but not critical drift. A retraining
schedule of every six months is recommended, with monthly monitoring of population stability
indices (PSI) to detect significant distribution shifts that would trigger an unscheduled retraining
cycle.

\section{Limitations and Challenges}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Dataset representativeness}: The model was trained on a synthetic dataset
          generated to mirror real FinTech data. Performance on a live platform dataset may
          differ; recalibration and transfer learning may be required.

    \item \textbf{Causal vs.\ correlational}: SHAP attributions reveal which features are
          predictive of churn but do not establish causal mechanisms. Interventions based on
          correlated signals (e.g., a customer who opened fewer push notifications) may be less
          effective than those addressing root causes.

    \item \textbf{Voluntary vs.\ involuntary churn conflation}: The churn label in the dataset
          does not distinguish between voluntary departure and involuntary closure (e.g., fraud
          cases). A refined label incorporating account closure reason codes would improve
          intervention targeting.

    \item \textbf{Cold-start problem}: Customers with fewer than 90 days of account history lack
          rolling-window features. A separate, simpler model trained on demographic and early
          behavioural signals is recommended for new customers.
\end{itemize}

\section{Comparative Analysis}

XGBoost consistently outperforms all other evaluated algorithms on both statistical and
business-oriented metrics. LightGBM achieves nearly equivalent performance at approximately
3$\times$ faster training speed, making it the preferred choice for production environments with
strict retraining time budgets. Logistic Regression, while substantially less accurate, offers
the advantage of direct regulatory auditability and may be the required choice in jurisdictions
where black-box models are disallowed for credit-related decisions.

The performance gap between tree-based ensemble methods and the MLP neural network underscores
the well-established finding that, for moderately sized tabular datasets, gradient boosting
typically outperforms deep learning---a result consistent with the survey by Grinsztajn et
al.~\cite{grinsztajn2022why}, who demonstrated that tree-based models outperform neural networks
on 45 of the 45 tabular benchmarks tested.
