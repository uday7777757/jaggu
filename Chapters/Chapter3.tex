\chapter{System Analysis}

\section{Analysis of Existing Practice, Proposed Solution, and Requirements}
Before proposing a machine learning solution, it is important to understand how churn is usually approached in many organizations and why those methods are insufficient in a competitive FinTech environment. Existing churn monitoring approaches are often descriptive rather than predictive. Business teams typically use reports showing monthly active users, transaction counts, dormant accounts, failed payments, complaint totals, and customer support summaries. While such reports are useful for observing trends, they do not estimate future behavior at the individual customer level. In practice, a manager may know that activity is falling overall, but may still be unable to answer a more important operational question: which specific customers are most likely to leave in the near future and why.

Another limitation of current practice is excessive reliance on manually created rules. For example, a business may classify customers as risky if they have not logged in for 30 days, have made fewer than three transactions in a month, or have submitted two complaints in one quarter. These rules are easy to understand, but they are not adaptive and they often fail to capture interactions among variables. A customer with medium transaction activity and rapidly increasing complaints may be more likely to churn than a customer with low transaction activity but long tenure and high balance, yet simple rules may treat them identically. As customer behavior becomes more digital and multidimensional, rule-based segmentation becomes too coarse for accurate risk prediction.

The proposed system addresses these issues by introducing a structured machine learning pipeline for customer churn prediction. The system begins with data ingestion from customer records, transaction logs, service interaction data, and related sources. Once loaded, the data is cleaned and transformed through preprocessing operations such as missing-value treatment, outlier control, encoding of categorical features, and scaling of numerical variables where necessary. This step is essential because machine learning models are sensitive to data quality, and raw operational data frequently contains inconsistencies. After preprocessing, feature engineering is performed to derive behavior-oriented indicators such as transaction intensity, complaint rate, and tenure categories. These engineered features help the model learn meaningful patterns that may not be obvious from the raw fields alone.

The next component of the proposed system is model training and selection. Instead of assuming a single best algorithm in advance, the system compares multiple models including Logistic Regression, Decision Tree, Random Forest, and XGBoost. This comparative strategy follows best practices from churn prediction research because no single model dominates in all datasets \cite{systematicreview,imani2024}. Logistic Regression provides a baseline and a degree of interpretability. Decision Trees expose decision paths clearly. Random Forest improves generalization by aggregating multiple trees. XGBoost offers strong predictive power through gradient boosting and is particularly effective on structured tabular data. Hyperparameter tuning through grid search or randomized search is used to improve each model’s performance.

An important analytical requirement in this project is class imbalance handling. In most churn datasets, non-churners are much more numerous than churners. If this imbalance is ignored, the resulting model may appear accurate while failing to detect the very customers the business wants to retain. The system therefore includes support for methods such as SMOTE and class-weighted learning. Evaluation is based on accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score, ROC-AUC, and confusion matrix analysis, with particular emphasis on recall and F1-score for the churn class. These metrics help ensure that the selected model is useful for decision-making rather than merely impressive on paper.

The hardware and software requirements of the system are modest, which makes the project suitable for academic implementation. A machine with at least 8 GB RAM, adequate storage, and a modern processor is sufficient for experimentation on the assumed dataset size. On the software side, Python provides the development foundation, while libraries such as pandas, NumPy, scikit-learn, matplotlib, seaborn, and XGBoost support the data science pipeline. A lightweight web framework such as Flask can be used to create a simple dashboard for viewing risk categories. Overall, the system analysis shows that the proposed solution is technically feasible, methodologically sound, and clearly superior to static, rule-based customer retention monitoring.
