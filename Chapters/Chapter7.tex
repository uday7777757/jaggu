\chapter{Results and Discussion}

\section{Overview of Results}
The illustrative performance comparison shows that ensemble models outperform simpler baseline models on the churn prediction task. Logistic Regression and Decision Tree provide useful interpretability, but their recall and F1-score are lower than those of Random Forest and XGBoost. XGBoost achieves the strongest overall balance of accuracy, churn recall, F1-score, and ROC-AUC in the example evaluation.

\section{Interpretation of Findings}
The results align with the literature reviewed in Chapter 2. Churn behavior is influenced by multiple nonlinear interactions among tenure, product usage, complaint history, and account characteristics. Ensemble methods capture these relationships more effectively than linear or shallow single-tree models. The improved recall of XGBoost is especially important because churn management systems should minimize missed high-risk customers even if this increases false positives moderately.

\section{Feature Analysis}
Based on the assumed feature importance pattern, \texttt{TenureMonths}, \texttt{NumTransactions}, and \texttt{Complaints} are expected to be among the strongest predictors. Customers with short tenure, low activity, and repeated complaints are more likely to churn. This interpretation is practical because it maps directly to retention actions such as onboarding assistance, re-engagement campaigns, and service recovery initiatives.

\section{Business Impact}
The practical value of the system lies in targeted retention. Instead of applying generic campaigns to the entire customer base, the business can focus on the segment with the highest predicted churn probability. Even a modest improvement in retention can produce substantial financial gains, as highlighted in churn management literature \cite{systematicreview}. A prioritized dashboard also allows customer success or marketing teams to work more efficiently with limited outreach capacity.

\section{Limitations}
The present study has several limitations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The dataset is assumed rather than drawn from a live institutional source.
    \item The numerical results shown are illustrative and must be replaced by real experimental outputs for final validation.
    \item Churn patterns may change over time because of concept drift.
    \item The strongest model, XGBoost, is less interpretable than simpler baselines unless paired with explainability tools.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion Summary}
Overall, the findings support the use of machine learning, especially ensemble methods, for FinTech churn prediction. The report demonstrates how predictive modeling can be connected to business interpretation, visual reporting, and operational risk categorization rather than remaining a purely academic classification exercise.
