\chapter{Literature Survey}

\section{Review of Literature, Methods, and Research Gap}
The study of customer churn has received sustained attention in fields such as telecommunications, banking, subscription services, insurance, and e-commerce because customer retention is strongly connected to profitability. In recent years, churn prediction has become especially relevant to FinTech platforms, where digital products generate large volumes of customer interaction data that can be mined for predictive insight. The literature in this area shows a clear evolution from simple statistical models to more sophisticated machine learning and ensemble-based approaches. At the same time, researchers have increasingly recognized that model performance depends not only on algorithm choice, but also on data preprocessing, class imbalance handling, feature engineering, evaluation design, and interpretability.

The first major theme in the literature is the definition and operationalization of churn. Imani and Arabnia describe churn as the cessation or significant decline of customer engagement with a service and show that the exact operational meaning differs across domains \cite{systematicreview}. In a telecom setting, churn may mean number portability or service discontinuation; in banking or FinTech, it may involve account closure, subscription cancellation, transactional inactivity, or migration to another platform. This variation matters because the prediction target must match business reality. A poorly defined churn label can produce misleading training data and weak practical usefulness. For this reason, many studies stress that data labeling should be grounded in domain knowledge and aligned with customer lifecycle behavior.

Another major theme is model selection. Traditional approaches such as Logistic Regression remain common because they are simple, computationally efficient, and relatively easy to interpret. Decision Trees also remain popular in academic work because they provide intuitive rule-based explanations and can expose non-linear behavior. However, modern comparative studies increasingly report stronger performance from ensemble methods. Imani et al. compare Random Forest and XGBoost under different imbalance-handling techniques and show that these methods often produce better precision-recall trade-offs than single-model baselines, especially when churners are a minority class \cite{imani2024}. Their work reinforces an important consensus in the literature: for structured tabular data, boosted and bagged tree-based models are often among the strongest practical choices.

The literature also highlights the central role of preprocessing. Bhattacharjee et al. emphasize categorical encoding and standard scaling as important steps in churn prediction pipelines, noting that improperly encoded features can reduce the effectiveness of downstream models \cite{bhattacharjee2026}. This is particularly relevant in FinTech datasets, where variables such as subscription type, region, activity category, and account status are categorical by nature. Converting these variables into machine-readable form through one-hot encoding or related techniques is essential. Likewise, missing values and outliers can distort model learning if not handled carefully. Numeric features such as account balance or transaction amount often contain skewed distributions that require thoughtful preprocessing rather than blind normalization.

Class imbalance is another recurring concern. Most churn datasets contain far fewer churners than retained customers, which means that accuracy can become a misleading metric. A model predicting every customer as retained may still appear highly accurate in a skewed dataset, while being useless for retention action. For this reason, the literature strongly recommends the use of precision, recall, F1-score, ROC-AUC, and confusion matrix analysis instead of relying only on accuracy \cite{systematicreview,imani2024}. Oversampling methods such as SMOTE and adaptive synthetic sampling, along with class-weighted training strategies, are frequently used to improve minority-class detection.

Feature engineering is treated in the literature as a decisive factor rather than an optional enhancement. Researchers repeatedly report that raw demographic variables alone are insufficient for strong churn prediction. Better performance is typically achieved when engagement measures, complaint frequencies, tenure-based indicators, average transaction values, and support interaction patterns are incorporated into the model. In FinTech specifically, tenure, inactivity, complaint history, and declining transaction activity often emerge as important predictors. Malhotra et al. show that even when artificial neural networks are used, the underlying value of the system still depends heavily on the quality and relevance of the input features \cite{malhotra2017}.

Recent literature also emphasizes explainability. While ensemble models offer stronger performance, business teams often require reasons behind a prediction before taking action. This need has motivated the use of explainable AI methods such as SHAP and LIME. These tools help identify the factors contributing most strongly to a given prediction, which improves trust and makes it easier to translate analytics into real interventions. Even when explainability tools are not fully implemented, the literature suggests that feature importance ranking and business-friendly risk bands are useful practical compromises.

Despite these advances, several research gaps remain visible. Many benchmark studies are still telecom-centered rather than FinTech-centered, meaning that the specific behavioral patterns of financial app users are less richly documented. In addition, several papers focus heavily on algorithmic performance while giving relatively little attention to deployment design, dashboard presentation, or operational integration into CRM systems. Some studies report strong metrics but do not discuss the decision threshold selection required in a real business environment. There is also ongoing need for work that balances predictive power, interpretability, and practical system architecture. The present project addresses these needs by combining multi-model comparison, structured preprocessing, feature engineering, risk categorization, dashboard-oriented outputs, and system design documentation in a single major-project report.
