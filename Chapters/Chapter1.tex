\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Background and Motivation}

The global financial technology (FinTech) sector has experienced explosive growth over the past
decade, driven by the proliferation of smartphones, ubiquitous internet connectivity, and a
generational shift in consumer expectations. Digital-first banking applications, peer-to-peer
payment platforms, robo-advisory wealth management tools, and buy-now-pay-later credit solutions
have collectively disrupted traditional financial services, lowering barriers to entry and
intensifying competitive pressure among service providers~\cite{provost2013data}.

In this hyper-competitive landscape, customer retention has emerged as a paramount strategic
concern. Customer churn---the voluntary or involuntary termination of a relationship between a
customer and a service provider---directly erodes the subscriber base and, consequently, recurring
revenue streams. Research in consumer behaviour consistently demonstrates that the cost of
acquiring a new customer is between five and twenty-five times higher than the cost of retaining
an existing one~\cite{ngai2009application}. Moreover, a mere five-percent increase in retention
rate can produce a profit uplift of twenty-five to ninety-five percent, depending on the industry
segment~\cite{haneen2021machine}.

Traditional approaches to churn management in FinTech organisations have relied heavily on
reactive strategies---engaging customers only after they have already signalled their intention to
leave, for example by submitting an account closure request. Such approaches are inherently
inefficient: by the time a customer articulates dissatisfaction, the probability of successful
retention through standard incentive campaigns is substantially diminished. The paradigm shift
towards data-driven, proactive churn prediction leverages the vast volumes of transactional and
behavioural data generated by digital financial platforms to identify at-risk customers weeks or
months before they defect.

Machine learning (ML) has proven particularly well suited to this task. Unlike rule-based expert
systems, ML algorithms can discover complex, non-linear decision boundaries in high-dimensional
feature spaces without requiring an analyst to manually specify predictive rules. Ensemble methods
such as Random Forests, Gradient Boosting Machines, and Extreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost) have
repeatedly demonstrated state-of-the-art performance on structured tabular data, which constitutes
the primary data modality in retail banking and FinTech applications~\cite{xgboost2016}.

Despite the technical maturity of these methods, practical deployment of churn prediction systems
in production FinTech environments faces several non-trivial challenges. These include: severe
class imbalance (churners typically represent fewer than twenty percent of the active customer
base); the heterogeneity and sparsity of raw transactional data; the need for model
interpretability to satisfy regulatory requirements and engender business trust; and the
operational complexity of integrating a live prediction service into existing customer relationship
management (CRM) and marketing automation workflows.

This project addresses all of the above challenges by designing and implementing a comprehensive,
production-ready churn prediction pipeline for a representative FinTech platform. The work spans
the entire machine-learning life cycle, from data ingestion and exploratory analysis through
feature engineering, model training and selection, hyperparameter optimisation, explainability
analysis, and REST API deployment.

\section{Problem Statement}

FinTech platforms accumulate enormous quantities of customer interaction data---account
transactions, login events, product uptake records, support ticket histories, and satisfaction
survey responses---yet the majority of this data remains under-exploited from a retention
management perspective. Without a systematic predictive framework, retention teams are forced to
rely on ad hoc indicators and subjective judgement to identify customers warranting proactive
outreach, resulting in suboptimal resource allocation, missed interventions, and unnecessary
campaign expenditure on customers who would not have churned regardless.

More specifically, the problem addressed in this report can be stated as follows:

\begin{quote}
\textit{Given a historical dataset of customer demographic attributes, transactional records,
product usage patterns, and support interaction histories for a FinTech platform, design and
implement a supervised machine-learning system that can accurately identify, at least thirty days
in advance, which currently active customers are at elevated risk of churning within the
subsequent ninety-day observation window, and provide actionable, interpretable explanations for
each prediction.}
\end{quote}

The secondary problem is to evaluate the business value of such a system by quantifying the
potential revenue retained through targeted intervention, expressed as return on investment (ROI)
relative to the cost of campaign execution.

\section{Objectives}

The objectives of this project are enumerated below:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item To perform a systematic literature survey of machine-learning approaches to customer
          churn prediction with particular emphasis on FinTech and banking applications, class
          imbalance mitigation strategies, and explainability methods.

    \item To design and implement a robust data preprocessing pipeline capable of handling
          missing values, outliers, data-type inconsistencies, and categorical encoding in a
          heterogeneous FinTech customer dataset.

    \item To engineer a rich set of predictive features---including recency--frequency--monetary
          (RFM) metrics, rolling-window behavioural statistics, and product-diversification
          indices---that capture the multidimensional nature of customer engagement.

    \item To evaluate seven supervised classification algorithms (Logistic Regression, Decision
          Tree, Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, XGBoost, LightGBM, and MLP) under consistent
          experimental conditions, using five-fold stratified cross-validation and a comprehensive
          suite of performance metrics.

    \item To mitigate class imbalance using SMOTE combined with Tomek link under-sampling, and
          to quantify the performance improvement attributable to this technique.

    \item To optimise the hyperparameters of the best-performing model via Bayesian search and
          report the resulting gains in ROC-AUC and F1-score.

    \item To provide model-level and prediction-level explanations using SHAP values, identifying
          the key drivers of churn risk.

    \item To quantify the business impact of the proposed system by estimating revenue retention,
          campaign efficiency, and ROI.

    \item To containerise the trained model and expose a RESTful prediction API suitable for
          integration with CRM platforms.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Scope and Assumptions}

\subsection{Scope}

The scope of this project encompasses the following:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Data scope}: The dataset used for model development comprises anonymised records
          of approximately 50{,}000 customers observed over a twenty-four-month period. The data
          includes demographic attributes, account metadata, monthly transactional aggregates,
          digital engagement metrics, and support interaction counts.

    \item \textbf{Modelling scope}: The project focuses exclusively on binary classification
          (churn vs.\ retain) rather than multi-class churn severity prediction or survival
          analysis. The prediction horizon is defined as ninety days from the scoring date.

    \item \textbf{Technology scope}: The implementation uses Python 3.10 with the scikit-learn,
          XGBoost, LightGBM, imbalanced-learn, SHAP, pandas, NumPy, and Matplotlib/Seaborn
          libraries. Model serving is implemented via a FastAPI application containerised with
          Docker.

    \item \textbf{Evaluation scope}: Model evaluation is conducted on a twenty-percent
          hold-out test set drawn from the most recent six months of data to simulate a realistic
          temporal split. Business-impact calculations assume an average customer lifetime value
          of \$1{,}200 and an intervention campaign cost of \$15 per targeted customer.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Assumptions}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Churn is defined as the absence of any revenue-generating transaction over a continuous
          ninety-day window following the observation date.
    \item Customer records are assumed to be independently and identically distributed, subject to
          the temporal train--test split.
    \item The dataset is assumed to be representative of the broader customer population; no
          external validation on a separate platform dataset is performed.
    \item Feature values are assumed to be available at the time of scoring with a maximum
          latency of twenty-four hours for aggregated features.
\end{itemize}

\section{Organisation of the Report}

The remainder of this report is structured as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Chapter 2 -- Literature Survey}: Reviews prior academic and industry research on
          churn prediction, covering traditional ML models, ensemble and boosting methods, deep
          learning approaches, class imbalance handling, feature engineering, evaluation metrics,
          and explainability techniques.

    \item \textbf{Chapter 3 -- System Analysis}: Examines the limitations of existing churn
          detection systems, presents the proposed system overview, specifies the dataset, and
          describes the preprocessing and feature engineering approach.

    \item \textbf{Chapter 4 -- System Design}: Documents the system architecture, data flow
          diagrams, UML class diagram, sequence diagram, activity diagram, and deployment
          architecture.

    \item \textbf{Chapter 5 -- System Implementation}: Details the technology stack, data
          loading and preprocessing code, feature engineering implementation, model training
          procedure, and hyperparameter tuning.

    \item \textbf{Chapter 6 -- Testing and Evaluation}: Presents the testing methodology,
          evaluation metrics framework, test case results, and analytical visualisations.

    \item \textbf{Chapter 7 -- Results and Discussion}: Summarises experimental results,
          analyses feature importance, quantifies business impact, discusses model performance
          insights and limitations.

    \item \textbf{Chapter 8 -- Conclusion and Future Work}: Synthesises key findings, confirms
          achievement of objectives, articulates contributions, and outlines directions for future
          research.
\end{itemize}
