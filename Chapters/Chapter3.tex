\chapter{System Analysis}

\section{Existing Systems and Limitations}

Most FinTech organisations currently employ one of three approaches to churn detection:

\subsection{Rule-Based Threshold Systems}

The simplest deployed approach defines churn risk as a binary flag triggered when a customer
satisfies a set of manually defined conditions, such as ``no login for 45 days AND fewer than 2
transactions in the past month''. While these rules are easy to implement and explain, they suffer
from several fundamental shortcomings:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Low recall}: Rules capture only the subset of churners who match the specific
          threshold pattern; churners with atypical behavioural trajectories are systematically
          missed.
    \item \textbf{Manual maintenance}: As customer behaviour evolves and new products are launched,
          rules must be manually revised by domain experts, creating maintenance overhead.
    \item \textbf{No probabilistic output}: Binary flags do not allow prioritisation among at-risk
          customers; retention resources cannot be allocated proportionally to risk magnitude.
    \item \textbf{No feature interaction}: Rules cannot capture compound signals (e.g., the
          combination of declining balance \emph{and} increasing support contacts, neither of
          which is alarming in isolation).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Traditional Logistic Regression Scorecards}

Many financial institutions deploy logistic regression scorecards---essentially weighted linear
combinations of predictor variables mapped to a probability output. While these improve upon
rule-based systems by producing a continuous risk score and handling multiple features
simultaneously, they are constrained by the linearity assumption and typically require manual
binning (coarse classification) of continuous predictors before entry into the model. The binning
process discards information and requires periodic recalibration to remain valid as data
distributions shift.

\subsection{Periodic Batch Scoring Without Retraining}

Even where ML models are deployed, a common limitation is the use of a single model trained
on historical data that is applied to new customers without scheduled retraining. As the customer
population and product catalogue evolve, model performance degrades---a phenomenon known as
concept drift. Without monitoring and retraining pipelines, the production model progressively
loses its discriminative power.

\subsection{Summary of Existing System Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Inability to capture non-linear feature interactions.
    \item Absence of systematic class-imbalance correction.
    \item Limited feature engineering depth, particularly absence of temporal rolling statistics.
    \item No automated model explainability for regulatory compliance.
    \item No closed-loop retraining mechanism.
    \item Disconnection between churn scores and downstream CRM action workflows.
\end{itemize}

\section{Proposed System Overview}

The proposed system addresses each limitation identified above through a modular, production-ready
pipeline comprising six integrated components:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Data ingestion layer}: Scheduled extraction of raw data from the FinTech
          platform's operational databases via a Python-based ETL pipeline.

    \item \textbf{Preprocessing engine}: Automated imputation, outlier treatment, encoding, and
          normalisation applied consistently to both training and scoring datasets.

    \item \textbf{Feature store}: A curated repository of forty-seven engineered features
          computed from raw inputs, including RFM metrics, rolling-window statistics, and ratio
          features.

    \item \textbf{Model training and selection module}: Seven classifiers evaluated under
          stratified cross-validation with SMOTE preprocessing; the champion model is selected
          by ROC-AUC and serialised to disk.

    \item \textbf{Inference API}: A FastAPI service that accepts customer identifiers and returns
          churn probability scores with SHAP-based feature attributions.

    \item \textbf{CRM integration layer}: Webhook-based delivery of scored customer cohorts to
          the marketing automation platform for campaign execution.
\end{enumerate}

The system supports both batch scoring (nightly runs for the full active customer base) and
real-time scoring (on-demand prediction for individual customers triggering high-risk events).

The raw features fall into six categories:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Demographics} (5 features): Age, gender, geographic region, income band,
          employment status.
    \item \textbf{Account metadata} (4 features): Account tenure (months), product type,
          account tier, registration channel.
    \item \textbf{Transactional aggregates} (8 features): Monthly transaction count, monthly
          transaction value, average transaction value, count of large transactions (above
          \$500), international transaction flag, ATM withdrawal count.
    \item \textbf{Digital engagement} (5 features): Monthly mobile app logins, monthly web
          logins, feature adoption count, push notification open rate, referral count.
    \item \textbf{Financial health} (4 features): Outstanding balance, credit limit, credit
          utilisation ratio, number of missed payments in the past twelve months.
    \item \textbf{Customer support} (2 features): Support ticket count in the past ninety days,
          mean resolution time (hours).
\end{itemize}

\section{Data Preprocessing Pipeline}

The preprocessing pipeline transforms raw input data into a clean analytical dataset suitable
for model training. The pipeline is implemented as a scikit-learn \texttt{Pipeline} object,
ensuring identical transformations are applied at both training time and scoring time.

\subsection{Missing Value Imputation}

Missing values account for 3.2\% of the dataset, distributed across five columns. Numerical
features with missing values are imputed using the median of the training-set column (robust to
outliers). Categorical features with missing values are imputed with the mode. Missing value
indicator flags are added as binary features to preserve the information that a value was absent.

\subsection{Outlier Treatment}

Outliers in numerical features are identified using the inter-quartile range (IQR) method:
values falling below $Q_1 - 1.5 \times \mathrm{IQR}$ or above $Q_3 + 1.5 \times \mathrm{IQR}$
are Winsorised (clipped) to the respective boundary values. This prevents extreme observations
from distorting the learned decision boundaries without discarding data.

\subsection{Categorical Encoding}

Low-cardinality categorical features (fewer than ten unique levels) are one-hot encoded. The
high-cardinality geographic region feature (156 unique values) is target-encoded: each level is
replaced by its within-level mean churn rate, computed on the training set and applied to the
test set to prevent target leakage.

\subsection{Normalisation}

Numerical features are standardised to zero mean and unit variance using the \texttt{StandardScaler},
a prerequisite for the Logistic Regression and MLP classifiers, and beneficial for distance-based
algorithms. Tree-based models are insensitive to monotone transformations, so standardisation is
applied uniformly for consistency.

\section{Feature Engineering Approach}

Feature engineering transforms the preprocessed raw features into a richer representation
space. The following derived features are constructed:

\subsection{RFM Metrics}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Recency}: Number of days elapsed since the customer's most recent
          revenue-generating transaction.
    \item \textbf{Frequency}: Total number of transactions in the most recent 90-day window.
    \item \textbf{Monetary}: Total transaction value in the most recent 90-day window.
    \item \textbf{RFM composite score}: Percentile-ranked combination of the three RFM
          dimensions, weighted by their respective regression coefficients in a univariate
          logistic regression.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Rolling-Window Statistics}

For each of the eight transactional aggregate features, rolling means and standard deviations
are computed over 30-day, 60-day, and 90-day windows, yielding 48 rolling statistics. Temporal
trend features---the slope of the linear regression fitted to monthly transaction counts over the
preceding six months---capture whether a customer's engagement is accelerating or decelerating.

\subsection{Ratio and Interaction Features}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Credit utilisation ratio: outstanding balance / credit limit.
    \item Support contact intensity: support tickets per month of account tenure.
    \item Digital engagement rate: mobile logins / (mobile logins + web logins).
    \item Product adoption index: number of distinct products used / total products available.
    \item Payment stress indicator: binary flag for outstanding balance exceeding 80\% of
          credit limit combined with at least one missed payment.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Time-Based Features}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Account age decile: tenure binned into ten equal-width deciles.
    \item Quarter of account opening: captures seasonality in cohort behaviour.
    \item Months since last product uptake.
\end{itemize}

After feature engineering, the full analytical dataset contains 75 columns: 28 raw features
(post-encoding) and 47 engineered features. A subsequent feature importance analysis (using
gradient boosting feature importance and mutual information scores) is used to identify and
optionally remove near-zero-variance and highly correlated features before model training.
