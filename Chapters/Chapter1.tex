\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Introduction, Motivation, and Scope}
Customer churn prediction has become an important application area of machine learning because it directly addresses one of the most expensive operational problems faced by service-oriented businesses. In the context of a FinTech platform, churn refers to the situation in which a customer stops using digital banking, wallet, payment, lending, investment, or related financial services for a prolonged period, closes the account completely, or moves to a competing platform. Unlike one-time transactional businesses, FinTech companies depend strongly on continuity of usage, trust, and recurring interaction. A reduction in active users does not only affect present revenue; it also weakens lifetime customer value, increases marketing expenditure, and reduces the efficiency of product growth strategies. For this reason, churn analysis has moved from being a purely descriptive business exercise to a predictive analytics problem where organizations aim to identify risky customers before the actual churn event occurs.

Traditional business monitoring methods usually rely on monthly reports, static dashboards, and manually defined heuristics such as low login frequency, repeated complaints, or a fall in transaction volume. These methods can provide useful high-level insight, but they do not support precise customer-level intervention. Two customers may show the same decline in transaction activity for very different reasons; one may be temporarily inactive while another may be on the verge of permanent attrition. Human-designed rules are often too rigid to capture these differences. Machine learning is well suited to this situation because it can learn patterns from historical customer behavior and identify complex combinations of features associated with churn. Instead of relying on a single indicator, a predictive model can use demographics, tenure, balance trends, transaction volume, complaint history, subscription details, and service activity together to estimate the likelihood of churn.

The motivation for this project is therefore both academic and practical. From an academic point of view, customer churn prediction is a strong real-world classification problem involving preprocessing, feature engineering, imbalance handling, algorithm comparison, and performance evaluation. It provides an opportunity to apply data science principles in a domain where decision-making has visible business impact. From a practical point of view, a reliable churn prediction system allows FinTech managers to prioritize retention campaigns, personalize communication, improve customer support allocation, and make better use of limited marketing budgets. If a company can identify even a small subset of high-risk customers early, it can intervene with onboarding support, service recovery, discounts, reminders, or relationship-building actions that may significantly improve retention outcomes.

The present project focuses on the design of a machine learning based customer churn prediction framework for a FinTech platform. The report assumes the availability of an anonymized customer dataset with structured attributes such as customer age, gender, region, account balance, credit score, number of transactions, account tenure, complaint count, active status, subscription type, and a target variable indicating whether the customer churned. Since a live institutional dataset is not provided in this report context, the dataset is treated as assumed but realistic, following the structure commonly described in published churn studies \cite{malhotra2017,systematicreview}. The size is assumed to be about 10{,}000 records, which is large enough to support model training, validation, and testing for a major academic project.

The main objective of the work is to build a classification pipeline that can divide customers into churn risk categories such as High, Medium, and Low. To achieve this, the study considers multiple supervised machine learning algorithms, namely Logistic Regression, Decision Tree, Random Forest, and XGBoost. These models are selected intentionally to cover a spectrum from simple and interpretable baselines to stronger ensemble methods with higher predictive power. The study also emphasizes proper preprocessing because raw customer data frequently contains missing values, imbalanced classes, categorical features, and scale differences between variables. Feature engineering is included as a major component because customer behavior is often better represented through derived measures such as average transactions per month, complaint frequency per tenure period, and tenure-based customer grouping than through raw columns alone.

The scope of the project is limited to offline predictive modeling, conceptual system design, and report-based presentation of results. It does not attempt production deployment, real-time event scoring, live API integration, or controlled retention experimentation, although these are discussed in the future work section. The report also does not include code listings, because the primary purpose here is to produce a formal major project document. Even so, the methodology is described in enough detail to show how the system would be implemented in practice using Python, scikit-learn, XGBoost, and a lightweight dashboard framework such as Flask. The remaining chapters present the literature foundation, system analysis, architecture, implementation approach, evaluation strategy, illustrative results, and future extension possibilities for the proposed churn prediction system.
