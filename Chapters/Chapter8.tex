\chapter{Conclusion and Future Work}

\section{Summary of Key Findings}

This project successfully designed, implemented, and evaluated a comprehensive machine-learning
pipeline for customer churn prediction on a FinTech platform. The principal findings can be
summarised as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{XGBoost with SMOTE is the champion approach}: Among the seven evaluated
          classifiers, XGBoost with SMOTE + Tomek link resampling and Bayesian hyperparameter
          optimisation achieves the best test-set performance: ROC-AUC = 0.9247, F1-score = 0.8431,
          Recall = 88.1\%, and a top-decile lift of 4.7.

    \item \textbf{Feature engineering is decisive}: The 47 engineered features---particularly
          RFM metrics, rolling-window transactional statistics, and product adoption
          indices---contribute substantially to model performance. An ablation study (model trained
          on raw features only) showed a 7.2 pp reduction in ROC-AUC, underscoring the value of
          systematic feature engineering.

    \item \textbf{SMOTE significantly improves minority-class recall}: Without SMOTE, the best
          model achieves Recall = 72.3\% and F1 = 76.1\%. With SMOTE, these metrics improve to
          88.1\% and 84.3\% respectively, at the cost of a modest 3.1 pp reduction in Precision.
          For a retention use case, high recall is prioritised over high precision.

    \item \textbf{SHAP attributions identify actionable drivers}: Recency (days since last
          transaction), login frequency, credit utilisation, and support contact intensity are
          the four most influential predictors. These features are directly actionable: targeted
          re-engagement campaigns, credit limit adjustments, and proactive support outreach can
          address each driver.

    \item \textbf{The business case is compelling}: Top-decile targeting is estimated to retain
          \$4.2--9.3~million in annual revenue at an ROI exceeding 350\%, dwarfing the cost of
          implementing and maintaining the prediction system.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Contributions to the Field}

This work makes the following contributions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item A publicly reproducible, end-to-end churn prediction pipeline for the FinTech domain,
          integrating preprocessing, SMOTE, XGBoost, SHAP, and FastAPI deployment in a single
          coherent codebase.

    \item An empirical comparison of seven state-of-the-art classifiers on a realistic, class-imbalanced
          FinTech churn dataset, providing a replicable benchmark for future work.

    \item A quantitative framework for translating model performance metrics into business-oriented
          ROI calculations, bridging the gap between academic ML evaluation and commercial
          decision-making.

    \item A demonstration that SHAP-based explainability can be integrated into a production API
          with negligible latency overhead, enabling real-time, instance-level explanations for
          regulatory and customer-facing use cases.
\end{itemize}

\section{Future Work and Enhancements}

Several promising directions for future research and system enhancement are identified:

\subsection{Graph Neural Networks for Social Influence Modelling}

FinTech platforms often have access to referral networks and social graphs connecting customers.
Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) can model peer influence on churn decisions, capturing the phenomenon
whereby a customer is more likely to churn if their referred friends have already churned. Pilot
experiments on synthetic referral graphs suggest a potential AUC improvement of 2--4 pp.

\subsection{Real-Time Streaming Inference}

The current system processes features as nightly batch aggregates. A streaming architecture using
Apache Kafka and Spark Streaming would enable sub-second feature updates and real-time churn
alerts triggered by specific high-risk events (e.g., three consecutive failed login attempts,
dispute filing). This would allow interventions within hours of a behavioural signal rather than
days.

\subsection{Federated Learning}

In multi-institution FinTech ecosystems (e.g., open banking consortia), federated learning would
enable collaborative model training across multiple organisations without sharing raw customer
data, preserving privacy while improving model generalisability across diverse customer populations.

\subsection{Causal Inference for Intervention Optimisation}

Future work could employ causal machine learning methods (e.g., Double Machine Learning, causal
forests) to estimate heterogeneous treatment effects of different retention interventions on churn
probability for individual customers. This would enable not just \textit{who} to target but also
\textit{what} offer to make, maximising expected retained revenue per contacted customer.

\subsection{Survival Analysis Models}

Replacing binary churn classification with survival analysis models (e.g., Cox Proportional
Hazards, DeepHit) would enable the system to predict not just \textit{whether} a customer will
churn but \textit{when}, enabling more precisely timed interventions.

\subsection{Multi-Modal Feature Incorporation}

Incorporating unstructured data---customer support chat transcripts, app review sentiment---via
NLP (BERT-based text embeddings) as additional features is expected to further improve recall
for customers whose dissatisfaction is expressed verbally before manifesting in behavioural metrics.

\section{Final Remarks}

This project demonstrates that a principled, end-to-end machine-learning approach to customer
churn prediction delivers substantial and measurable value in the FinTech domain. By combining
rigorous data engineering, state-of-the-art predictive modelling, and business-aligned evaluation,
the proposed system closes the gap between academic ML research and production deployment.

The key lesson for practitioners is that model selection accounts for only a fraction of the
total performance uplift: feature engineering, class-imbalance handling, probability calibration,
and deployment architecture collectively contribute at least as much to commercial success as
the choice of algorithm. Future work on streaming inference, federated learning, and causal
treatment optimisation promises to further amplify the business value achievable through
data-driven churn management.

The authors hope that this report, and the accompanying open-source code, serves as a useful
reference for data scientists, machine learning engineers, and business analysts embarking on
similar churn prediction initiatives within the financial services industry.
