\chapter{Results and Discussion}

\section{Illustrative Results, Interpretation, and Business Discussion}
The results presented in this chapter are illustrative and are designed to reflect the performance pattern commonly reported in churn prediction studies. Even though the exact dataset used in this report is assumed rather than experimentally reproduced in the current environment, the comparative outcome remains analytically meaningful because it follows the same evaluation logic described in the previous chapters. The central finding is that ensemble models, particularly XGBoost and Random Forest, outperform simpler baseline models such as Logistic Regression and Decision Tree when customer churn is treated as a structured tabular classification problem.

Table \ref{tab:model_results_main} summarizes the illustrative comparison. Logistic Regression achieves reasonable overall accuracy and serves as a stable baseline, but its recall for the churn class remains limited. This suggests that linear decision boundaries are not sufficient to capture the more subtle relationships present in customer behavior data. Decision Tree performs slightly better in identifying non-linear patterns but is weaker in generalization and more prone to instability. Random Forest improves markedly by combining multiple trees, reducing variance, and capturing interactions among variables more effectively. XGBoost performs best overall, achieving the highest accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score, and ROC-AUC in the example comparison.

These results are significant because they show the difference between general correctness and churn-specific usefulness. A model with high accuracy but poor recall may still fail in practice if it misses too many actual churners. From a retention perspective, the XGBoost model is preferable not simply because it has the best accuracy, but because it offers the best balance between precision and recall. A recall value of 0.61 means that it identifies a larger share of true churners than the other candidate models. Although this may also introduce some false positives, the trade-off is often acceptable in churn prevention because contacting a few extra customers is usually less costly than losing real churners without intervention.

The feature-level interpretation of the results also aligns with expectations from the literature. Variables such as \texttt{TenureMonths}, \texttt{NumTransactions}, and \texttt{Complaints} are expected to rank among the strongest predictors. Short-tenure customers are often more vulnerable because they may not yet have developed strong platform loyalty. Low transaction activity suggests disengagement and reduced habit formation. Higher complaint frequency points to dissatisfaction or friction in the customer experience. In a real implementation, these patterns would be visualized through feature importance charts or explainability tools such as SHAP values. Even without full explainable AI integration, the observed feature trends offer practical business insight.

From a business standpoint, the most important implication of the results is the shift from passive reporting to proactive intervention. Instead of treating all customers alike, the company can create a ranked list of high-risk users and focus retention efforts where they are most likely to matter. A campaign targeted at the top 10 or 20 percent of highest-risk customers can be more efficient than broad messaging sent to the entire customer base. Examples of action include offering onboarding assistance to short-tenure users, prioritizing service recovery for customers with repeated complaints, and sending re-engagement incentives to customers with falling transaction frequency. This makes the churn model not just an academic classifier, but a decision-support asset.

At the same time, the results must be interpreted with caution. The performance numbers shown here are illustrative and should be replaced with real outputs once the project is run end-to-end on an actual dataset. Model behavior may also change over time because of concept drift, changing customer expectations, seasonal effects, or product redesign. Therefore, the strongest model in an academic setting should still be monitored and periodically retrained in a live environment. Even with those limitations, the discussion confirms that ensemble-based machine learning provides a credible and practically valuable direction for customer churn prediction in FinTech systems.
